Research the location

Before setting out, it is important to understand the terrain and likely lighting conditions at the location. Spend some time researching it online. I use Google Maps to explore potential photography locations and understand how they can be accessed.

Remember to check the weather forecast too. Understanding the landscape in advance can help you anticipate useful viewpoints, the direction of the light and possible compositions before you arrive.

Equipment

Having suitable equipment can make landscape photography considerably easier. You do not need to bring everything you own, but the following items are worth considering.

  • Lenses: Wide-angle lenses are often preferred for landscape photography because they allow you to include more of the scene. A focal length between approximately 14 mm and 35 mm can work well for broad landscapes. A telephoto lens is also useful when you want to isolate distant details or compress the visual distance between elements. A standard kit zoom is perfectly suitable when you are beginning.
  • Filters: Lens filters can help control light and reflections. A circular polarising filter can reduce reflections and increase colour saturation, particularly when photographing water or the sky. ND filters allow longer exposures, which can smooth moving water or turn moving clouds into streaks. Mist and diffusion filters can soften bright highlights and create a more dreamlike appearance, although they are a creative option rather than an essential item.
  • Tripod: A tripod is one of the most useful pieces of equipment in landscape photography, even if many photographers consider carrying and setting one up a necessary evil. It keeps the camera stable and helps preserve image sharpness, particularly during longer exposures. Choose one that can support your camera and lens securely without looking as though it may collapse when you touch it. #TeamHamaStar

Camera settings

To create strong landscape photographs, it helps to understand how the main camera settings affect the result. Thanks, Roland! Here are a few useful starting points.

  • Aperture: The selected aperture affects the depth of field. For landscapes, a narrower aperture such as f/8 to f/16 can help keep more of the scene acceptably sharp. At extremely narrow settings, however, diffraction may slightly reduce overall image quality.
  • Shutter speed: Choose the shutter speed according to the available light and the creative effect you want. A slower shutter speed can show movement in flowing water, clouds or foliage. A faster shutter speed can freeze motion and preserve sharpness in windy conditions.
  • ISO: Keep the ISO as low as practical to reduce noise and preserve image quality, particularly when there is plenty of light. As conditions become darker, you may need to raise it to maintain a usable exposure. In most situations, increasing the ISO should be the final adjustment after considering the aperture and shutter speed.
  • White balance: When photographing in RAW format, the white balance can be adjusted freely during editing. If you are not already using RAW, this is a good reason to begin. For JPEG photographs, select a preset that suits the current lighting conditions and check whether the colours appear natural.
  • Focusing: The best focus position depends on the scene, focal length, aperture and distance between the foreground and background. A common starting point is to focus approximately one-third of the way into the scene rather than automatically choosing the most distant background. Manual focus and image magnification can help when you want to check the important areas precisely.

Safety comes first

Even when the main goal is creating impressive photographs, safety should always come first. Wear comfortable footwear and clothing suitable for hiking and changing weather. Carry a map, compass or GPS-enabled device for navigation, particularly in an unfamiliar area.

Bring enough water, remain hydrated and understand your physical limits. The strongest photographs are rarely worth an injury or becoming stranded after dark. You will make better decisions when you are safe, comfortable and properly prepared.

Creative inspiration

Experiment and allow yourself to try unfamiliar approaches. Explore different viewpoints, use natural elements in the foreground or include a person to show the scale of the landscape and create a stronger human connection.

When you need inspiration, Instagram and Pinterest can provide useful starting points, although it is worth identifying exactly what appeals to you rather than copying a photograph directly. You can also explore this landscape photography collection on 500px.

Final thoughts

Landscape photography offers an enormous range of possibilities. With a little research, suitable preparation and enough time to explore the location, I hope your first landscape photography trip will be both productive and enjoyable.
